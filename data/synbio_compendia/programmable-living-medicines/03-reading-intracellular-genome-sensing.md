# Reading II: Intracellular Genome, Transcriptome, and Epigenome Sensing

> **Filed under:** Programmable Living Medicines > Part III
> **Created:** 2026-06-09
> **Last updated:** 2026-06-09

## Summary

The first half of "reading" (Part III, prior chapter) covered what a cell can perceive at its *surface*. This chapter is the frontier half: how a living therapeutic cell can read its own — or a target cell's — **actual genotype, transcript, and epigenetic state from the inside**, where the defining lesions of cancer and clonal disease actually live. The pivotal conceptual move, and the organizing thesis of the chapter, is to stop trying to **bind** the variant base and instead make the cell **report** it: detection events are transduced into the expression of an arbitrary payload, so a cell manufactures a beacon *because of its own genotype*. This is exactly what ADAR-based RNA sensors (CellREADR, RADARS/RADAR — three independent estates) and mismatch-intolerant Cas13 already do in living cells with single-base resolution, leak floors near 1–3% and dynamic ranges of tens- to ~277-fold (Qian et al., 2022; Kaseniit et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Shembrey et al., 2024). [The ~277-fold figure is from Jiang et al. 2023, not Kaseniit et al.] By contrast, imaging a genomic-DNA single-nucleotide variant in a *live* cell at flow-cytometry throughput is essentially unsolved — a consequence of the 0.34 nm-in-3.2 Gb problem — and remains a fixed-cell, amplification-dependent specialty (Wang et al., 2022; sgGOLDFISH). From first principles, the transcriptome and epigenome each carry only a few bits per locus that a synthetic sensor can recover, and that channel is corrupted by allele-specific expression, nonsense-mediated decay, and ADAR's own A-to-I background — confounds that set hard, per-variant feasibility limits rather than platform-level ones.

## Background / First Principles

### The information in a locus, and what a synthetic sensor can actually recover

A somatic point mutation is, in the abstract, **2 bits** — one of four bases at one position. But a therapeutic sensor never has access to those 2 bits directly. It has access to whatever physical channel carries a *shadow* of that base into a place a synthetic molecule can act: the transcript, the displayed peptide, or the chromatin/methylation state. Each channel imposes its own information bottleneck.

- **Transcript (mRNA) channel.** A heterozygous somatic SNV produces a transcript pool that is, at best, ~50% mutant message. The discriminating signal is therefore not "is the base an A or a G" but "can a sensor reject the wild-type allele that is abundant in *every* cell while firing on the mutant allele present in only some cells." The mutual information between sensor output and true genotype collapses if wild-type cross-reactivity is non-trivial, because the wild-type transcript is the dominant species genome-wide. This is the deepest reason single-base RNA sensing is hard: the noise floor is not random — it is a high-copy, one-mismatch decoy present in the same and neighboring cells.
- **Peptide-MHC channel.** Covered in the surface-reading chapter and in the sibling immunology report; it is HLA-restricted and display-dependent, reading the *protein*, not the variant base.
- **Epigenetic channel.** A CpG methylation state is nominally ~1 bit (methylated/unmethylated) per cytosine, but disease-relevant epigenetic identity is encoded in *patterns* across hundreds of CpGs — a regional, probabilistic code, not a single switch. No live-cell synthetic sensor reads 5mC directly today; methylation is read by destructive assays (bisulfite/EM-seq) or, indirectly, by sensing the *transcriptional consequence* of an epigenetic state. This is a recurring theme of the CHIP and epigenetics reports in this library: the epigenome is informationally rich but synthetically illegible in vivo.

The first-principles punchline: **the transcriptome is the only channel that is simultaneously (a) variant-specific, (b) readable in living cells, and (c) programmable to an arbitrary output.** That is why every buildable genotype-sensing actuator routes through RNA.

### Why you report rather than bind

A chromophore that physically binds a single genomic base would have to win a thermodynamic discrimination contest — the free-energy gap between a perfectly matched probe and a single-mismatch duplex — against body-temperature noise, inside a chromatinized, protein-occluded nucleus, at single-copy abundance. Toehold and competitive-displacement designs achieve this in vitro, but the live nucleus defeats them. The escape is to replace *binding* with *catalytic reporting*: an enzyme (ADAR, Cas13) performs a sequence-specific reaction only when its guide finds the cognate target, and that reaction gates the synthesis of an amplifiable output. The cell becomes its own signal amplifier. This is the same conceptual inversion that the sibling report [genotype-directed cytotoxicity](../opto-car/seed-report.md) identifies as "the single most important conceptual move" — and this chapter develops the *reading* mechanics that report depends upon, while that report develops the *actuation* and clinical program built on top.

## Key Findings

### 1. ADAR sensors — co-opting the cell's own editing enzyme as a single-base detector

The dominant live-cell RNA-sensing chassis hijacks **ADAR** (adenosine deaminase acting on RNA), an enzyme every human cell already expresses. The architecture, shared in essentials across three independently invented families:

- A single sensor RNA carries a 5′ **sensor/guide region** complementary to the target transcript and a 3′ **payload ORF**. An in-frame **UAG STOP codon** sits within the sensor region.
- When the sensor base-pairs with its cognate cellular RNA, the resulting double-stranded RNA recruits endogenous ADAR, which deaminates the adenosine of the UAG (A→I). Inosine is read by the ribosome as guanosine, so **UAG → UIG → read as UGG (tryptophan)**: the STOP is erased and the downstream payload is translated (Qian et al., *Nature* 2022; Kaseniit et al., *Nat Biotechnol* 2023).
- The payload is arbitrary: a fluorophore, a recombinase, a caspase, diphtheria toxin A, a surface tag, or a luciferase. Detection of a transcript is thereby converted into expression of any protein you choose, **inside an unmodified living cell**, with no exogenous protein required (the editing enzyme is the host's).

The three estates (a naming trap worth stating once, because they are routinely confused):

- **CellREADR** — Z. Josh Huang lab, **Duke** (Qian et al., *Nature* 2022). Sensor domain ~300 nt; STOP placed near the center; demonstrated cell-type targeting in mouse and human tissue.
- **Gao-lab sensor** (Xiaojing Gao lab, **Stanford**; Kaseniit et al., *Nat Biotechnol* 2023), licensed to **Radar Therapeutics**; the group's later protocol (Katz et al., *Nat Protoc* 2025) brands the system **RADARS**. This paper demonstrated discrimination of disease-relevant point mutations and fusions (e.g., distinguishing TP53 R248W vs R248Q), plus AND/OR logic. [Note: the "RADARS" acronym is used by both this lineage and the MIT/Broad paper below — the two are routinely confused; the published acronym "Reprogrammable ADAR Sensors (RADARS)" was coined in Jiang et al. 2023.]
- **RADARS** (Reprogrammable ADAR Sensors) — Abudayyeh/Gootenberg/Fei Chen, **MIT/Broad** (Jiang et al., *Nat Biotechnol* 2023), granted as **US 12,195,734 B2**. Systematic sensor engineering in this paper achieved up to **~277-fold** activation. Same year, same principle, different patent estate. (See the IP teardown in the [sibling report](../opto-car/seed-report.md), §12.)

**The CCA edit-context design rule.** ADAR is not sequence-agnostic about *where* it will edit. Decades of substrate studies show a strong nearest-neighbor preference: ADAR disfavors a 5′ guanosine adjacent to the target adenosine (5′ preference U > A > C > G) and favors a 3′ G (3′ preference G > C ≈ A > U) (Eggington et al., 2011; Kuttan & Bass, *PNAS* 2012, which mapped these neighbor preferences to the deaminase catalytic domain and a conserved loop — residues E488/T490/V493 — rather than to direct base recognition; a specific steric clash with a 5′ guanosine is sometimes attributed to a flipping-loop glycine but this exact mechanistic detail is [unverified] against the primary structural literature). In practice this means the editable adenosine of the sensor STOP performs best in a **trinucleotide context approximating UAG with a favorable flank** — operationally, sensor designs exploit a triggering **C–A mismatch** at the edit site, and the design heuristic that the target supply an effective **CCA** at the editing position. The consequence for variant sensing is concrete and constraining: **the discriminating base must be placed at or adjacent to the editing-critical position**, where a single mismatch maximally perturbs editing. The design rule that follows — build the sensor complementary to the *mutant* allele so the *wild-type* allele creates a mismatch at the edit site and fails to fire — is developed in the sibling report (§8a). Not every somatic SNV falls in a compatible context. This is a per-variant feasibility gate, not a universal capability.

**Leak and fold-change are the binding physical limits.** ADAR sensors have a non-zero **basal leak** — some payload translated without target — because (i) ADAR can occasionally edit the STOP in the absence of perfect target pairing, and (ii) translational readthrough of UAG is itself non-zero. First-generation CellREADR and RADARS reported leak on the order of low single-digit percent of the "on" signal, with engineered fold-changes from ~tens-fold up to ~277-fold (the ~277-fold figure from Jiang et al., 2023). For a *cell-fate-deciding* application — mark this cell for death — even 1–3% leak across a large wild-type compartment is a population-level specificity problem, which is exactly why downstream logic gates (AND across two variants; physical-contact gating) matter. Leak is the noise floor; fold-change is the signal ceiling; their ratio is the usable channel capacity.

### 2. Cas13 — ADAR-independent single-base sensing by engineered mismatch intolerance

A complementary route avoids host ADAR entirely. **Cas13** is an RNA-guided RNA-targeting nuclease whose collateral and on-target activity is, in its native state, somewhat *mismatch-tolerant*. The 2024 advance (Shembrey et al., *Sci Adv* 2024, "Principles of CRISPR-Cas13 mismatch intolerance...") showed that **introducing synthetic mismatches at defined positions in the crRNA spacer** converts Cas13 into a single-base discriminator: the engineered guide pairs productively only with the mutant transcript, leaving the wild-type allele intact. They demonstrated SNV-selective, dose-dependent silencing of **KRAS G12 variants, NRAS G12D, and BRAF V600E**, sparing the cognate wild-type message. A useful side effect: the mismatch-bearing crRNAs *reduced* the promiscuous collateral activity of RfxCas13d.

Why this matters as a *sensing* chassis rather than a silencer:
- It is **ADAR-independent**, removing the dependence on a host enzyme that cancers actively tune to evade interferon immunity (ADAR1 manipulation is a known immune-evasion axis). A Cas13 sensor cannot be blinded by a clone downregulating ADAR.
- Cas13 can be **repurposed from "degrade the mutant" to "report the mutant"** — e.g., a dCas13 or a Cas13-coupled circuit that releases a payload upon mutant-transcript recognition. This is less mature than the ADAR-sensor payload route, but it is the cleaner long-term substrate where host-ADAR confounds (below) are severe.

### 3. The confounds that corrupt the RNA channel — and why they are biology, not engineering

Three biological facts degrade the mutual information between an RNA sensor's output and the true somatic genotype. None is fixable by better engineering alone; each must be designed *around*.

**(a) Allele-specific expression (ASE).** Transcription is not allele-symmetric. Cis-regulatory variants, imprinting, X-inactivation, and stochastic monoallelic expression mean the mutant and wild-type alleles of a gene are frequently transcribed at *different* rates within the same cell. A sensor calibrated on the assumption "heterozygous ⇒ ~50% mutant message" can misfire when ASE skews the ratio — silencing the mutant allele's expression dims a true positive; boosting it can raise apparent leak. ASE is genome-pervasive (a large fraction of expressed genes show measurable allelic imbalance) and is a first-order, under-modeled confounder for somatic-SNV RNA sensing.

**(b) Nonsense-mediated decay (NMD) — the silent enemy of loss-of-function sensing.** Truncating mutations (nonsense, frameshift) introduce premature termination codons that flag the transcript for NMD, destroying the very mutant mRNA the sensor needs. A substantial minority of NMD substrates escape (last-exon and very-5′ PTCs are spared), and NMD efficiency is in fact highly variable across transcripts, tissues, and individuals [the often-quoted "~one-third escape" is an approximation, not a fixed rate — unverified as a universal figure], so escape cannot be relied upon. The strategic consequence: **RNA sensing is strongest for missense and in-frame variants** (stable full-length transcript, one clean discriminating base) and **weakest for the truncating LOF lesions that dominate tumor-suppressor genes.** TET2 is the cautionary case — ~77% of TET2 lesions are truncating, exactly the NMD-susceptible class — making the gene that motivated this whole research arc one of the *harder* RNA-sensing targets (developed quantitatively in the [sibling report](../opto-car/seed-report.md), §8c). This is a target-selection problem, not a platform failure.

**(c) Endogenous A-to-I editing background.** Human transcriptomes contain millions of native A-to-I editing sites (the great majority in Alu repeats). For an ADAR-based sensor, the host's own editing activity is the operating environment: a transcript may *already* carry I where the sensor expects A, and global ADAR1 levels rise with interferon signaling — so the same inflammatory milieu that drives a CHIP clone (IL-1β, IL-6) also perturbs the sensor's enzyme supply. ADAR-independent (Cas13) sensing sidesteps this; ADAR sensing must treat host editing state as a covariate.

### 4. The unsolved problem: genomic-DNA SNV imaging in live cells at flow throughput

The most literal version of "read the genotype" — a probe that enters the nucleus, binds the variant *base in DNA*, and lights up for flow cytometry — is essentially unsolved, and the reasons are physical, not incremental.

- **The 0.34 nm-in-3.2 Gb problem.** A single base pair is ~0.34 nm of a ~3.2-billion-bp genome packaged into chromatin. Discriminating one substitution requires either an enzymatic specificity step plus signal amplification, or a hybridization probe whose match-vs-single-mismatch ΔG wins against thermal noise at 37 °C inside a protein-occluded nucleus. Neither survives the live-cell, single-copy, flow-throughput combination.
- **dCas9 live imaging works only at repeats.** Catalytically dead Cas9 fused to fluorophores (dCas9–GFP, dCas9–SunTag for amplification) can paint loci in *living* cells, but routinely only at **repetitive/high-copy** loci where many guides cluster signal. Single-copy and SNV-level discrimination generally require amplification and are demonstrated in **fixed** cells.
- **Single-nucleotide-sensitive imaging exists — but fixed-cell only.** Direct-hybridization genome imaging reached single-nucleotide sensitivity (Wang et al., *Nat Commun* 2022). **sgGOLDFISH** exploits a specificity-enhanced Cas9 whose cleavage is sensitive to single-base-pair differences to discriminate point mutations — it visualized allele-specific *LMNA* progeroid point mutations in patient fibroblasts (Nat Commun 2022) — but it depends on Cas9 cleavage of fixed genomic DNA, not a live-cell readout. **CasPLA** (Cas9-mediated proximity ligation) visualizes SNVs in single cells via amplification, again in fixed material.

**The verdict:** there is no published method for genomic-DNA SNV discrimination in *live* cells at flow-cytometry throughput. The literal "variant-specific nuclear flow cytometry" is, today, a fixed-cell, amplification-dependent specialty. This is precisely why the field — and any buildable therapeutic — routes through RNA reporting rather than DNA binding. The transcript is the legible shadow of the genotype; the genotype itself stays dark in a living nucleus.

## Counterintuitive & Groundbreaking Applications

**1. The reporter is a manufactured molecule, so the readout can be anything — including a therapeutic decision.** Because an RNA sensor's output is *translated*, not *bound*, the "fluorophore" can be swapped for a recombinase that permanently records the genotype call (a genetic write-once memory of "this cell was JAK2-V617F-positive at time t"), a surface tag for a universal CAR, a membrane luciferase for trans-cellular BRET, or a caspase. The sensing chapter and the actuation program of the [sibling report](../opto-car/seed-report.md) meet here: reading *is* the first half of a programmable kill. The counterintuitive consequence is that the *hardest* part of "genotype-directed therapy" is not the killing — it is rejecting the high-copy wild-type transcript that is present in every cell.

**2. Sensor multiplexing turns escape into a fitness tax.** A clone can evade an RNA sensor by acquiring a *silent/synonymous* base change in the sensor footprint that breaks pairing without altering the driver protein. The counter is to tile multiple sensors with OR-logic across the minimal SNV footprint, so no single neutral mutation blinds all of them — and because the target *is* the oncogenic driver, escaping by losing the target means losing the fitness advantage (the driver-dependence trap developed in the sibling report, §11). Reading the driver, not a dispensable surface antigen, is structurally harder to escape.

**3. An ex vivo "genotype-gated flow" diagnostic is buildable today.** Transduce a marrow/blood sample with a variant-specific ADAR sensor whose payload is a fluorophore; cells expressing the mutant transcript light up; sort and sequence to confirm. This is single-cell, genotype-resolved cytometry achieved by *induced expression* rather than direct binding — sub-$100K, no patient risk, and it de-risks the entire downstream therapeutic stack by answering the only make-or-break question (can the sensor reject the abundant wild-type transcript) before any optics or CAR engineering is attempted. The recommended validation cell-line panel and metrics (HEL homozygous, SET-2 heterozygous, K562 wild-type controls; FACS-then-sequence; VAF tracking zygosity in the sorted fractions) are specified in the [sibling report](../opto-car/seed-report.md), §12 staging.

**4. The epigenome is the white space.** No live-cell synthetic sensor reads methylation patterns directly. The frontier move is *indirect*: sense the transcriptional consequence of an epigenetic state (e.g., a gene silenced or de-repressed by a CHIP-associated methylation change) and treat that as a proxy for the epigenome. This couples the synthetic-sensing program to the methylation-clock and CHIP-progression work elsewhere in this library — a methylation pattern that no probe can read becomes legible through the transcripts it controls.

## Open Questions

- **Can a single ADAR/Cas13 sensor reject a high-copy, ubiquitous one-mismatch wild-type transcript at population scale?** This is the unproven crux. Single-cell discrimination demos exist; whole-population specificity at therapeutic precision (no wild-type compartment lit) is not established.
- **How low can leak go without sacrificing fold-change?** The leak/fold-change trade-off sets channel capacity; whether second-generation sensors can push leak below ~1% while keeping >50-fold induction is open.
- **What is the true per-variant feasibility map under the CCA/edit-context rule?** No systematic catalog exists of which recurrent somatic SNVs sit in ADAR-compatible contexts versus which require Cas13 or are inaccessible.
- **Does ASE meaningfully degrade somatic-SNV calling in primary cells?** Allele-specific expression is genome-pervasive but its quantitative impact on RNA-sensor accuracy in primary disease cells is unmeasured.
- **Is there any live-cell route to genomic-DNA SNV reading?** A non-amplified, live, flow-compatible DNA-variant readout would change the field — but the physics (0.34 nm in 3.2 Gb) suggests it may be fundamentally barred without an enzymatic catalysis-plus-amplification step, i.e., it converges back to reporting.
- **Can the epigenome ever be read synthetically in vivo, or only through its transcriptional shadow?** No direct live-cell 5mC sensor exists; whether one is buildable is open.
