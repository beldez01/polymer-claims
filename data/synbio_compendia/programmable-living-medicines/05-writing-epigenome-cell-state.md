# Editing the Epigenome and Controlling Cell State

> **Filed under:** Programmable Living Medicines > Part V
> **Created:** 2026-06-09
> **Last updated:** 2026-06-09
> **Tags:** epigenetic-editing, CRISPRoff, dCas9-DNMT3A, partial-reprogramming, OSK, Waddington, quasi-potential, T-cell-exhaustion, TOX, CAR-T-persistence, TET2, DNMT3A, CHIP, hysteresis, bistability, cell-state

## Summary

This chapter argues that the most therapeutically consequential "write" in cell therapy is the one that **changes no base of DNA at all**. Epigenome editors — catalytically dead Cas9 fused to DNA-methyltransferase or demethylase domains, of which CRISPRoff/CRISPRon is the canonical instance (Nuñez et al., Cell 2021) — install heritable transcriptional states by hit-and-run deposition of CpG methylation and repressive histone marks, with silencing persisting through hundreds of cell divisions and even through differentiation, without ever inducing a double-strand break (DSB). The deeper claim is conceptual: a cell's **state** is a self-reinforcing attractor on a quasi-potential landscape, stabilized by epigenetic hysteresis, and the engineer's job is not to flip a single bit but to **reshape the landscape** — to lower the barrier out of a pathological basin or raise the wall around a therapeutic one. This reframes three otherwise unrelated problems — partial reprogramming/rejuvenation (OSK), T-cell exhaustion as an epigenetically *fixed* fate (TOX, de novo DNMT3A methylation), and the TET2/DNMT3A cofactor axis of clonal hematopoiesis — as a single discipline of **state control**. The most counterintuitive consequence: you may be able to therapeutically neutralize a clonal-hematopoiesis driver by **erasing its epigenetic fitness advantage rather than killing the cell**, and you can make a CAR-T cell durable by *writing* a stem-memory program into it rather than selecting for one.

## Background / First Principles

### Why "state" is a different object than "sequence"

A genome is a string; a cell state is an **attractor**. The same ~20,000 genes, identical in every somatic cell, produce a neuron, a hepatocyte, and a long-term hematopoietic stem cell (LT-HSC) because each cell occupies a different self-stabilizing configuration of transcription-factor occupancy, chromatin accessibility, and DNA methylation. Waddington's 1957 metaphor of a marble rolling down a landscape of branching valleys is, it turns out, formalizable: gene-regulatory networks with mutual repression and positive autoregulation generate **multistable dynamical systems**, and one can compute a **quasi-potential** U(x) — a Lyapunov-like function whose minima are the stable cell types and whose ridges are the differentiation barriers (Wang et al., PNAS 2011; reviewed in [../energy-landscape-hematopoiesis/_overview.md](../energy-landscape-hematopoiesis/_overview.md)). For non-equilibrium gene networks the potential is not a strict gradient (there is a rotational/flux component, Freidlin–Wentzell theory), but the topology — basins, barriers, saddle points — is real and is what an epigenetic engineer manipulates.

Two physical principles make state engineering tractable and durable:

1. **Bistability and hysteresis.** A bistable switch (e.g., a gene that activates its own transcription above a threshold) has *two* stable outputs for the *same* input over a range — the hallmark of hysteresis. To flip it you must push the input past a bifurcation point; once flipped, it stays flipped even when the input returns to baseline. CpG methylation is a near-ideal hysteretic memory: a methylated CpG is copied to the daughter strand by the maintenance methyltransferase DNMT1 at each replication (hemimethylated CpG → fully methylated), so the mark **templates its own inheritance**. This is why a transient editing pulse can yield a permanent state change — the *cell*, not the editor, maintains the memory.

2. **State is harder to fake-escape than a surface antigen.** This is the load-bearing therapeutic asymmetry. A tumor escapes CD19-CAR-T by simply ceasing to express a *dispensable* surface protein — antigen loss drives 30–70% of relapses ([../immunology/genotype-directed-cytotoxicity.md](../opto-car/seed-report.md), §11). But a cell cannot "drop" its differentiation state without ceasing to be the cell it is. If the therapeutic target *is* the pathological state itself (the exhaustion program, the stemness program, the inflammatory-macrophage program), escape requires reorganizing the whole attractor — a far higher-dimensional, more costly move than silencing one gene. State, in information-geometric terms, sits at a high-curvature point of the cell's identity manifold; it is expensive to leave and expensive to counterfeit.

### The molecular instruction set for writing state without cutting DNA

The reader/writer chemistry of the epigenome is covered in [../epigenetics/methylation-gene-regulation.md](../epigenetics/methylation-gene-regulation.md) and [../chip/02_methylation_biology.md](../chip/02_methylation_biology.md). The minimal vocabulary the rest of this chapter needs:

- **Writers:** DNMT3A/3B (de novo CpG methylation), DNMT1 (maintenance), and histone-modifying complexes (KRAB→TRIM28→SETDB1 deposits H3K9me3; PRC2/EZH2 deposits H3K27me3).
- **Erasers:** TET1/2/3 dioxygenases, which oxidize 5-methylcytosine (5mC) to 5-hydroxymethylcytosine (5hmC) and onward, enabling active and passive demethylation. TET activity is α-ketoglutarate- and Fe(II)-dependent — making it sensitive to metabolic state (2-hydroxyglutarate from mutant IDH is a competitive inhibitor), a key disease link.
- **The targeting chassis:** catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9), dCas12, or zinc-finger/TALE arrays fused to any of the above, programmed to a locus by a guide RNA. No nuclease activity, **no DSB, no indels, no genotoxic risk from on-target cutting.**

## Key Findings

### Epigenetic editors: durable, heritable silencing without a double-strand break

The defining demonstration is **CRISPRoff** (Nuñez et al., Cell 2021): a single fusion of dCas9 to a ZNF10 **KRAB** domain plus the **DNMT3A** catalytic domain and the **DNMT3L** stimulatory cofactor. The KRAB domain recruits TRIM28 and lays down H3K9me3; DNMT3A/3L methylates the local CpG island. Transiently expressed (the editor is delivered as plasmid or mRNA and then dilutes away), CRISPRoff initiates silencing that is **mitotically heritable for >450 cell divisions / ~50 days in culture**, is **maintained through the differentiation of iPSCs to neurons**, and — critically — works at the **majority of protein-coding genes, including those lacking canonical CpG islands** (Nuñez et al., Cell 2021). Its companion **CRISPRon** (dCas9–TET1) reverses the silencing by targeted demethylation, establishing a write/erase pair: the state is not just installed but **toggle-addressable**.

The mechanistic punchline — and the reason this is the chapter's centerpiece — is that **durability is DNA-methylation-dependent**. KRAB-only silencing (H3K9me3 alone) is transient; it relaxes within days once the editor is gone. Adding the DNMT3A module converts a transient repression into a self-templating, DNMT1-maintained heritable state (Nuñez et al., Cell 2021; "rules of heritability" dissected in O'Geen et al., Nucleic Acids Res 2022, who quantified that **DNA methylation, not H3K9me3 per se, is the determinant of long-term hit-and-run silencing**). This is bistability made molecular: the H3K9me3 mark is the *fast, leaky* variable; the CpG methylation is the *slow, latching* variable that holds the memory.

**The hit-and-run durability question — the central open problem.** Heritability is locus-, context-, and cell-type-dependent. Some loci silence and stay silenced; others (often those with strong, broadly used promoters, or in stem cells with active demethylation machinery) revert. Reactivation can occur if endogenous TET activity erodes the methylation faster than DNMT1 maintains it. For a *therapeutic* — where you want a clinically meaningful fraction of a cell population permanently reprogrammed in vivo — predicting and guaranteeing durability per-locus is unsolved. The honest framing: epigenetic editing's durability is **probabilistic and locus-dependent**, not deterministic, and that is the gap between the elegant 2021 demonstration and a dosed medicine.

**Why this beats sequence editing for many cell-therapy indications.** A CRISPR-Cas9 DSB to knock out a gene risks p53 activation, large deletions, chromosomal translocations, and chromothripsis; base/prime editors avoid the DSB but still permanently alter sequence and can have bystander edits. Epigenetic silencing is **reversible, dosage-tunable (you can hypomethylate to dial expression down rather than off), and sequence-preserving** — there is no permanent genomic scar and, in principle, an antidote (CRISPRon). For settings where the goal is to *modulate* a gene's output in an engineered cell rather than ablate it, this is a categorically safer write.

### Partial reprogramming: reshaping the landscape, not just flipping a switch

Yamanaka's four factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC = OSKM) drive a somatic cell **up and over** the differentiation barriers, all the way to induced pluripotency — the marble climbs back to the top of Waddington's hill. **Partial reprogramming** asks: what if you push *part way* up the slope and then stop, resetting age-associated epigenetic marks while preserving cell identity?

- **Cyclic OSKM in vivo** (Ocampo et al., Cell 2016): short, pulsed OSKM expression (2 days on / 5 days off) in a progeroid (LAKI) mouse ameliorated four hallmarks of aging — epigenetic alterations, senescence, DNA damage, mitochondrial dysfunction — and extended lifespan, **without inducing teratomas**, precisely because expression was transient. Continuous OSKM, by contrast, is lethal (teratomas, loss of identity). The *cyclic* protocol is the engineering insight: stay in the basin, lower its floor.
- **OSK (dropping the oncogene c-MYC) restores vision** (Lu et al., Nature 2020, 588:124–129): AAV-delivered OSK in retinal ganglion cells of aged or injured mice reduced DNA-methylation age, restored youthful transcriptional patterns, promoted axon regeneration after optic-nerve crush, and **reversed vision loss in glaucoma and aged-mouse models**. Reversal was **TET-dependent** — knocking down TET enzymes abolished the rejuvenation — proving the effect runs through active DNA demethylation, i.e., through the epigenome-writing machinery this chapter is about.
- **Targeted/transient partial reprogramming improves health markers** in physiologically aged (not just progeroid) mice (Browder et al., Nat Aging 2022; targeted approaches, Sci Transl Med 2023), broadening the result beyond a premature-aging artifact.

The conceptual unification: **partial reprogramming is landscape engineering.** OSK/OSKM do not target a single gene; they globally perturb the regulatory network and let TET-mediated demethylation reset the epigenetic "clock" (see [../chip/03_epigenetic_clocks.md](../chip/03_epigenetic_clocks.md) for the clocks being reset). The marble is nudged toward a younger, lower-energy version of *its own* basin. The therapeutic risk is the same as the conceptual mechanism: push too far and the cell loses identity or transforms (the teratoma cliff). Frontier-speculative, with real replication and safety questions, but the in-vivo vision-restoration result is robust enough to anchor a clinical program (multiple companies, e.g. Altos, NewLimit, are pursuing it).

### Cell-state control as therapy I: T-cell exhaustion as an epigenetically *fixed* fate

This is where epigenetic state control becomes immediately, mechanistically central to cell therapy. Chronic antigen stimulation — exactly what a CAR-T cell experiences against a solid tumor or a persistent leukemic burden — drives CD8⁺ T cells into **exhaustion**: progressive loss of effector function, upregulation of inhibitory receptors (PD-1, TIM-3, LAG-3), and metabolic collapse. The decisive discovery is that exhaustion is not merely a transcriptional adjustment but an **epigenetically enforced developmental fate**:

- **TOX is the master writer of the exhaustion program.** TOX (a HMG-box transcription factor) is induced by chronic TCR→calcineurin→NFAT signaling and then becomes self-sustaining in a calcineurin-*independent* feed-forward loop. TOX **transcriptionally and epigenetically programs** exhaustion: it reorganizes chromatin accessibility to commit the cell to the exhausted lineage (Khan et al., Nature 2019; Scott et al., Nature 2019; Alfei et al., Nature 2019, all 2019). Without TOX, exhausted T cells fail to form; robust TOX translates "persistent stimulation" into a distinct, locked transcriptional state. Continuous TOX is required to *safeguard* the exhausted epigenetic fate (Science Immunol 2024) — exhaustion is actively maintained, a stable attractor, not a passive decay.
- **De novo DNA methylation locks it in.** Exhaustion is enforced by **DNMT3A-mediated de novo CpG methylation** at loci controlling T-cell multipotency and effector recall (Ghoneim et al., Cell 2017). The exhausted state is, literally, a methylation-encoded basin.

The therapeutic inference is direct and counterintuitive: **delete or epigenetically disable the writer and you prevent the cell from entering the basin.** Prinzing et al. (Sci Transl Med 2021) showed that **DNMT3A knockout in CAR-T cells prevents exhaustion** — DNMT3A-null CAR-T cells preserved proliferative capacity and antitumor function through prolonged tumor exposure that exhausted wild-type cells. This is genomic-method cell-therapy engineering in its purest form: a single epigenetic-writer deletion reshapes the accessible landscape so the exhaustion basin is no longer reachable. The bistability/hysteresis framing predicts exactly this — if you remove the slow latching variable (de novo methylation), the system cannot hold the exhausted state and relaxes back toward effector/memory.

### Cell-state control as therapy II: writing stemness/memory for CAR-T persistence

The other half of the durability problem is the *positive* one — engineering a CAR-T cell that persists. Clinical response correlates strongly with the infusion product being **stem-cell-memory (T-SCM)/central-memory** rather than terminally differentiated effector. The serendipitous proof: in the famous CLL patient who entered durable remission from essentially a single CAR-T clone, the curative clone arose because lentiviral integration **disrupted *TET2***; the TET2-disrupted cells displayed an **altered-differentiation epigenetic profile and a central-memory phenotype** at the peak of a massive expansion (Fraietta et al., Nature 2018). One patient, but a profound natural experiment: a chance hit to an epigenetic *eraser* produced the persistence phenotype that the field engineers toward.

The synthesis with the exhaustion result is elegant and at first paradoxical:
- **TET2 loss** (eraser off → methylation accumulates) → enhanced proliferation/central-memory in one CAR-T context (Fraietta 2018).
- **DNMT3A loss** (writer off → de novo methylation blocked) → exhaustion resistance, preserved multipotency (Prinzing 2021).

These are not contradictory once you stop thinking "more methylation good / less methylation good" and think **landscape**: both manipulations *destabilize terminal differentiation/exhaustion basins and favor self-renewing, multipotent states*, just by perturbing different nodes of the same methylation machinery. TET2 disruption appears to uncouple proliferative from effector programs (Jain et al., Blood 2020 [conference abstract]; the matured finding was published as Jain et al., Nature 2023, "TET2 guards against unchecked BATF3-induced CAR T cell expansion"), which can be a feature (expansion) or a liability (the hyperproliferative, leukemia-adjacent phenotype seen in TET2-edited cells — a real safety flag). The engineering goal is therefore not "knock out a methylation gene" but **transiently bias the writing/erasing balance to deposit a stem-memory epigenome at manufacture**, then let it stabilize — a job for tunable epigenetic *editing* (locus-specific CRISPRoff/CRISPRon at exhaustion- and stemness-control loci) rather than blunt gene knockout.

### The TET2/DNMT3A cofactor axis and its disease relevance: CHIP

The same two genes that govern CAR-T fate are the **two most common drivers of clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP)** — DNMT3A and TET2 mutations together account for the majority of CHIP clones ([../chip/02_methylation_biology.md](../chip/02_methylation_biology.md), [../chip/04_progression_evolution.md](../chip/04_progression_evolution.md)). This is not a coincidence; it is the same biology read in a different cell. In HSCs, *TET2* loss-of-function produces global hypermethylation/hydroxymethylation imbalance, biases differentiation toward myeloid lineages, and confers a **competitive self-renewal advantage** — the clone expands not because it is malignant but because it sits in a slightly deeper, stickier stem basin than its wild-type neighbors. TET2-mutant macrophages are epigenetically rewired toward a hyperinflammatory phenotype (elevated IL-1β/IL-6; the CANTOS TET2 signal), detailed in [../epigenetics/tet2-macrophage-immunophenotype.md](../epigenetics/tet2-macrophage-immunophenotype.md). The TET–DNMT axis is, in effect, the **landscape-tuning knob of hematopoiesis**: gain or lose a writer/eraser and you re-slope the valleys that determine self-renewal vs. differentiation ([../energy-landscape-hematopoiesis/_overview.md](../energy-landscape-hematopoiesis/_overview.md) models this explicitly as a methylation→barrier operator on the quasi-potential).

## Counterintuitive & Groundbreaking Applications

### 1. Erase a CHIP clone's fitness epigenetically instead of killing it

The genotype-directed-cytotoxicity chapter ([../immunology/genotype-directed-cytotoxicity.md](../opto-car/seed-report.md), §7) reaches a hard verdict: you **cannot** simply kill all TET2-mutant cells in benign CHIP, because the clone is millions of *functional* HSCs and ablating them risks marrow failure. State control offers an entirely different exit. A TET2-mutant clone's advantage is **epigenetic** — a self-renewal bias encoded in its methylation landscape. If an epigenome editor (dCas9–TET1/CRISPRon or dCas9–DNMT3A) delivered to the clone could **re-slope its landscape to neutralize the competitive advantage** — restoring normal differentiation propensity at the loci where TET2 loss skews it — the clone would simply lose its edge and be out-competed back to baseline by wild-type HSCs, *without a single cell being killed*. This is "de-fitness-ing" rather than cytotoxicity: turn a fitness-enhancing state into a neutral one and let cell competition (an ally, per the JAK2 analysis in the sibling chapter) do the rest. The deep appeal: it sidesteps both the marrow-failure problem (no killing) and the escape problem (the cell can't drop a state it depends on without surrendering the very advantage you're erasing). The hard part is **clone-specific delivery of an editor in vivo** and **identifying the precise re-sloping loci** — both unsolved, but conceptually this is the cleanest therapeutic logic CHIP has been offered.

### 2. Reprogram the niche, not (only) the clone

A clone's fitness is relative to its microenvironment. The HSC niche — endothelial and mesenchymal stromal cells, the inflammatory cytokine milieu — sets the *shape* of the competitive landscape. TET2-mutant clones are favored partly because aging/inflammatory niches select for them (inflammation-adapted clones). The counterintuitive move: **epigenetically reprogram the niche** to remove the selective pressure (e.g., partial-reprogram or anti-inflammatory-reprogram the stromal/macrophage compartment) so the mutant clone is no longer favored — changing the landscape *under* the marble instead of pushing the marble. This connects partial reprogramming (niche rejuvenation), CHIP biology, and cell competition into one therapeutic frame and is, to my knowledge, an unexplored intersection.

### 3. Write durable stem-memory into CAR-T at manufacture with a transient epigenetic pulse

Rather than *selecting* for T-SCM cells or *knocking out* DNMT3A (a permanent, potentially hazardous edit given the leukemia-adjacent phenotypes of methylation-gene-null T cells), apply a **transient, locus-specific CRISPRoff/CRISPRon pulse** during the brief ex-vivo manufacturing window to install a stem-memory epigenome: silence exhaustion-program loci (e.g., TOX-driven nodes), de-repress stemness/self-renewal loci. Because hit-and-run silencing is heritable, the state propagates through the in-vivo expansion without persistent editor expression — the cell carries the memory, the editor is gone (and thus not a continuing safety liability). This is "epigenetic preconditioning" of a living drug and is the natural product realization of the Fraietta/Prince/Khan triad.

### 4. State-gated, not antigen-gated, targeting

If the pathological *state* (exhausted, senescent, hyperinflammatory-macrophage, leukemic-stem) is harder to fake-escape than a surface antigen, the highest-value targets for the sense→compute→act architectures of this treatise are **state signatures** read at the transcriptome/epigenome level. An RNA-sensing actuator (the ADAR/Cas13 platforms of the sibling chapter) tuned to a *state-defining transcript set* — rather than a single somatic variant — would be intrinsically more escape-resistant, because the cell must abandon the whole program to evade. The cost is that state signatures are graded, not binary; AND-gate logic over multiple state markers is required to recover specificity.

## Open Questions

- **Per-locus durability is unpredictable.** Which loci, in which cell types, hold a hit-and-run silencing state for a clinically meaningful duration in vivo? The "rules of heritability" are partial (O'Geen 2022); we cannot yet *design* for guaranteed durability. Endogenous TET activity vs. DNMT1 maintenance is the tug-of-war, and its setpoint varies by locus and cell state.
- **5hmC/5mC confound and the TET readout.** TET-dependent rejuvenation (Lu 2020) and TET2-CHIP biology both hinge on 5mC↔5hmC dynamics, but most assays (bisulfite) cannot distinguish them. The functional state may depend on the *intermediate* (5hmC), not just net demethylation — see [../epigenetics/methylation-biophysics.md](../epigenetics/methylation-biophysics.md).
- **Is the exhaustion basin truly reversible, or only preventable?** DNMT3A-KO *prevents* exhaustion; whether an editor can *reverse* an already-fixed, TOX-safeguarded exhausted state (the more clinically useful intervention — rescuing infused cells in vivo) is unsettled. Hysteresis predicts reversal requires crossing a different, possibly higher, barrier than prevention.
- **Off-target epigenome editing.** dCas9 fusions deposit methylation at off-target binding sites; unlike a sequence edit, a stray heritable epigenetic mark is harder to detect and could silence a tumor suppressor — a genotoxicity analog with no DNA-sequence footprint. Genome-wide methylation off-target profiling is immature.
- **In-vivo, clone-specific editor delivery.** Every "de-fitness the clone" and "reprogram the niche" application is gated by the same unsolved problem the sibling chapter flags: getting an editor into a specific cell population in marrow in vivo (LNP/AAV/lentiviral tropism).
- **The teratoma/transformation cliff.** Partial reprogramming and stem-memory writing both push toward de-differentiation; the safety margin between "rejuvenated/persistent" and "transformed" is the central unknown for clinical translation.
