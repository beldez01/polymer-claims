# Reading I: Surface and Antigen Sensing

> **Created:** 2026-06-09
> **Last updated:** 2026-06-09
> **Tags:** CAR, TCR, pMHC, TCR-mimic, antigen-density, kinetic-proofreading, synNotch, SNIPR, MESA, affinity-discrimination, HLA-restriction, signal-1-2-3, neoantigen, immune-synapse

## Summary

This chapter develops the *mature* half of how an engineered cell "reads" — it recognizes other cells by what they **display on their surface**. We build, from physical first principles, the molecular grammar of the two natural reading machines (the TCR and its synthetic descendant the CAR), the three-signal logic of T-cell licensing, and the central paradox of recognition: **above a certain affinity, sensing gets *worse*, not better** — because discrimination at the synapse is a kinetic, not a thermodynamic, computation. We then show how peptide–MHC (pMHC) turns the surface into a low-resolution window onto the *intracellular* proteome, how TCR-mimic antibodies and nanobody-CARs exploit that window to hit public intracellular oncoproteins (WT1, NPM1, KRAS G12D, TP53, HPV E6), and why HLA restriction is the hard ceiling on the whole approach — present in only ~27–50% of patients for the dominant allele HLA-A\*02:01 (Tano-Menka et al., 2023), and routinely lost by tumors as an escape route. Finally we cover the *programmable* surface-reading layer — synNotch, SNIPR, MESA — receptors that decouple "what is sensed" from "what is done," giving the cell a writable input port. The chapter closes by mapping the structural failure modes of surface sensing — loss-of-function lesions, intracellular non-displayed drivers, antigen escape — which are precisely the gaps that motivate the **intracellular reading** of Part III ([genotype-directed cytotoxicity](../opto-car/seed-report.md)).

## Background / First Principles

A cell that recognizes another cell faces an information-theoretic problem at a crowded, jiggling interface. The two membranes appose across a cleft of ~13–15 nm — the dimension set by the TCR–pMHC complex itself (Davis & van der Merwe, 2006). Into that cleft project thousands of receptor–ligand pairs, almost all of them *self*. The discriminating receptor must answer, in seconds, a question with an astronomically skewed prior: of ~10⁵ pMHC complexes a T cell scans on a target, perhaps **1–10 carry the cognate foreign/mutant peptide** (Sykulev et al., 1996; Irvine et al., 2002 showed as few as ~1–3 agonist pMHC can trigger a T cell). The signal-to-noise is therefore ~10⁴:1 *against* the answer being yes, and the receptor must nonetheless fire reliably on the handful of true ligands while ignoring the flood of near-identical self.

No equilibrium binding rule can solve this. If recognition were a thermodynamic readout — "bind tightly = activate" — then self-ligands that happen to bind even moderately would trigger, and autoimmunity would be universal. The biological solution is **kinetic**: the receptor measures *how long* a ligand stays bound (dwell time, τ = 1/k_off), not how tightly it binds at equilibrium (K_D). This is the foundation everything in this chapter rests on, so it is worth stating as a law:

> **Discrimination at the immune synapse is a measurement of bond lifetime processed through kinetic proofreading, not a measurement of affinity.**

**Kinetic proofreading** (McKeithan, 1995, adapting Hopfield's 1974 proofreading concept) posits that ligand binding initiates a *chain* of slow, energy-consuming modification steps (TCR ITAM phosphorylation by Lck, ZAP-70 recruitment and its own multi-step activation; Voisinne et al., 2022 *Nat Immunol* localized the dominant proofreading step to ZAP-70). The chain only reaches the firing threshold if the bond survives long enough to traverse all steps; if the ligand dissociates early, the intermediate decays back to baseline (the "reset"). Because the probability of completing N sequential steps depends *supralinearly* (≈ exponentially) on dwell time, a small difference in τ becomes a large difference in output — the molecular origin of sharp self/non-self discrimination. There is an **optimal dwell time** (Kalergis et al., 2001): too short and proofreading never completes; too long and a competing mechanism, **serial triggering** (Valitutti et al., 1995), saturates. Serial triggering is the counterpoint — a single pMHC, if it releases fast enough (high k_off), can sequentially engage and trigger *many* TCRs, amplifying a signal from very few ligands. Kinetic proofreading dominates at low affinity; serial triggering at high affinity; signal peaks in between (Lever et al., 2014). This non-monotonic "sweet spot" is the physics behind every affinity-tuning result later in this chapter.

The natural recognizer is the **αβ T-cell receptor (TCR)**, a disulfide-linked heterodimer whose membrane-distal V domains form the pMHC-binding surface but which carries **no intrinsic signaling capacity** — it transmits through the associated **CD3 complex** (CD3γε, CD3δε, CD3ζζ), contributing **10 ITAMs** across the assembly. This modular split — recognition (TCRαβ) separated from signaling (CD3) — is exactly the architecture synthetic biology would later hijack.

## Key Findings

### Signal 1, 2, 3: the licensing logic a CAR collapses and a TCR obeys

A natural T cell does not act on recognition alone. It integrates three signals:

- **Signal 1** — antigen: TCR engagement of cognate pMHC (specificity).
- **Signal 2** — costimulation: CD28 engaging CD80/CD86 on a *professional* antigen-presenting cell (licensing/"is this a sanctioned context?"). Signal 1 *without* Signal 2 drives **anergy** — a built-in safety interlock against autoreactivity.
- **Signal 3** — cytokine milieu (IL-12, IL-2, type I IFN), which programs the *quality* and fate of the response (effector vs memory; Th polarization).

The **chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)** is, from this vantage, a deliberate *short-circuit* of this logic. A second-generation CAR fuses an extracellular **scFv** (antibody recognition, MHC-independent) → hinge → transmembrane → a **costimulatory domain (CD28 or 4-1BB)** → **CD3ζ** (Signal 1) in a *single polypeptide* (June & Sadelain, 2018). It hardwires Signal 1 and Signal 2 into one chain triggered by one binding event, deleting the Signal-2 *checkpoint* — which is why CARs are potent and also why they have no native brake against recognizing antigen in the wrong context. The costimulatory choice tunes kinetics and persistence: **CD28** CARs signal fast and exhaust faster; **4-1BB** CARs signal slower, persist longer, and resist exhaustion (Long et al., 2015 traced 4-1BB's persistence advantage to reduced tonic-signaling exhaustion). Third-generation CARs stack two costimulatory domains; "armored"/TRUCK designs add an inducible **Signal-3 payload** (e.g., IL-12) the cell secretes on activation. The strategic point for this treatise: **a CAR is a hand-built sense→compute→act device where the "compute" is currently a single AND-less threshold** — the rest of this Part is about giving it real logic.

Crucially, because the CAR uses an antibody, it reads **native surface protein, not pMHC**, and is therefore **MHC-unrestricted** — its blessing (works in any patient regardless of HLA) and its curse (it can only see what is *already* on the surface; it is blind to the 99%+ of the proteome that lives inside the cell). That blindness is the gap pMHC sensing partially fills (next section) and that intracellular reading fully addresses (Part III).

### Antigen-density thresholding: recognition is digital, and the threshold is a designable parameter

A CAR does not respond to "present vs absent" but to **antigen density crossing a threshold** — and that threshold is one of the most therapeutically exploitable parameters in the field.

The numbers are concrete. For CD19 CAR-T in B-ALL, lymphoblasts expressing **below ~2,000 CD19 molecules/cell** are significantly less likely to respond (Majzner et al., 2020); in large-B-cell lymphoma a baseline of ~**3,000 CD19/cell** stratifies relapse risk. CD22 CAR-T illustrate the dark side: high initial response rates are followed by relapse driven by **antigen *downmodulation* below threshold** rather than complete antigen loss — the leukemia survives by dimming, not deleting (Fry et al., 2018). This is **antigen escape as a continuous variable**, and it is the dominant mechanism of CAR-T failure: 30–70% of relapses involve antigen loss or downregulation of the targeted *dispensable* surface protein.

The threshold is tunable because the **dose–response is sigmoidal (ultrasensitive)**, and its steepness and midpoint are engineerable. Hernandez-Lopez et al. (2021, *Science*) made the design principle explicit: a single CAR has a shallow, near-linear response that discriminates high- from low-density targets poorly; but a **two-receptor positive-feedback circuit** — a low-affinity synNotch sensing antigen drives expression of a high-affinity CAR against the *same* antigen — produces a steep sigmoid that sharply distinguishes tumor-level HER2 (high) from normal-tissue HER2 (low). Density-thresholding is thus how surface sensing *manufactures a therapeutic window from a self-antigen*: when a target is shared between tumor and vital tissue (HER2, the cautionary tale being the fatal lung toxicity of a high-affinity HER2 CAR; Morgan et al., 2010), demanding *high* density spares normal tissue.

### The affinity paradox: above the sweet spot, sensing gets worse

The single most counterintuitive — and most load-bearing — fact in surface sensing:

> **Increasing receptor affinity beyond an optimum does not improve and actively degrades discrimination.**

Empirically, a panel of HER2/ErbB2-specific CARs spanning a range of scFv affinities showed that **the activation threshold is *inversely* correlated with affinity**: high-affinity CARs fire against cells with *low* HER2 (losing the tumor/normal window), while lower-affinity CARs require *high* HER2 to fire and so confine killing to tumor (Liu et al., 2015, *Cancer Res*; reviewed Drent et al., 2017). The therapeutic optimum is therefore a *deliberately weakened* binder.

The mechanism returns us to first principles. Discrimination is a *kinetic* readout of dwell time. Push K_D too low (affinity too high) and three things happen: (1) k_off becomes so slow that **serial triggering collapses** — each receptor is occupied by one ligand it cannot release, so few ligands can no longer trigger many receptors; (2) the proofreading chain completes even for the *low-density, low-context* self-engagements you wanted to ignore, because the bond now always outlasts the proofreading delay; (3) the response saturates and *loses* its density dependence — the sigmoid flattens at the bottom, erasing the threshold that created selectivity. High affinity converts a tunable discriminator into a hair-trigger.

The deepest illustration is the **apparent contradiction of tebentafusp/ImmTAC**. The soluble ImmTAC reagent (an affinity-matured TCR fused to anti-CD3) targeting gp100/HLA-A\*02:01 was engineered to bind its pMHC with **~1-million-fold higher affinity than a natural TCR** — into the picomolar regime — and it works clinically, the first agent to extend overall survival in metastatic uveal melanoma (Nathan et al., 2021, *NEJM*; ~49% reduction in risk of death). Doesn't this refute the paradox? No — it *proves the kinetic framing*. ImmTAC is **not a cell** and does not rely on synapse-level proofreading to discriminate; it is a soluble bridge whose job is to *find* and *occupy* the rare pMHC stably, and discrimination is delegated to the specificity of the engineered binding interface plus the requirement to cross-link CD3. A **cellular** TCR or CAR cannot afford million-fold affinity, because in a cell, affinity that high *destroys* the kinetic discrimination the cell depends on. The lesson is precise: **affinity engineering is a soluble-reagent strategy; dwell-time tuning within the proofreading sweet spot is the cellular strategy.** Conflating them is a category error that has killed CAR programs.

### pMHC: the surface as a low-bandwidth window onto the intracellular proteome

Surface sensing seems doomed to the ~tens of % of the proteome at the membrane — until MHC class I. Every nucleated cell continuously samples its *intracellular* proteome: the proteasome degrades cytosolic proteins (including mutant oncoproteins, viral proteins, and lineage-restricted intracellular antigens) into peptides, TAP pumps them into the ER, and **MHC-I displays a representative ~8–11-mer cross-section on the surface** — on the order of ~10⁵–10⁶ total pMHC per cell across thousands of distinct peptides, meaning any one peptide may be present at only **single-to-low-double-digit copies** (Sykulev et al., 1996). This is the channel by which an *intracellular* genotype leaks to the *surface* as a peptide. It is the entire reason a "surface-reading" therapy can ever touch an intracellular driver — and its bandwidth limits define the failure modes below.

Two pMHC-targeting modalities are **FDA-approved**, proving the channel is real and clinically actionable: **tebentafusp** (ImmTAC, gp100/HLA-A\*02:01, uveal melanoma) and **afamitresgene autoleucel** (engineered-TCR cell therapy, MAGE-A4/HLA-A\*02:01, synovial sarcoma; approved 2024). Both are HLA-A\*02:01-restricted — foreshadowing the ceiling.

### TCR-mimic antibodies and nanobody-CARs: antibody affinity, TCR specificity, against public neoantigens

The pMHC window can be addressed without a TCR at all. **TCR-mimic (TCRm) antibodies** are antibodies (or nanobodies) raised/engineered to recognize a *specific peptide in the MHC groove* with antibody-grade affinity and TCR-grade peptide specificity, then deployed as CARs, bispecific engagers, or ADCs (reviewed Holland et al., 2024; Lozano-Rabella et al., 2024). The flagship is **ESK1**, a TCRm binding the WT1-derived **RMF** peptide on HLA-A\*02:01 at sub-nanomolar affinity, effective as both bispecific and CAR against WT1⁺ leukemias and solid tumors (Dao et al., 2013, 2015). The conceptual payoff is large: WT1, NPM1, TP53, and KRAS are **intracellular, often "undruggable," and frequently public** (recurrent across patients), so a pMHC-directed binder turns a private intracellular lesion into a shared surface target.

The target list is the most exciting frontier of surface sensing because it is the list of *driver* oncoproteins:
- **TP53** mutant/neoantigen pMHCs and **KRAS G12D/G12V** — public neoantigens from the most common solid-tumor drivers; TCR and TCRm programs are advancing (Hsiue et al., 2021, *Science*, built a bispecific against a mutant-p53 pMHC; Wang et al. and others against KRAS G12D/HLA-C\*08:02 and HLA-A\*11:01).
- **NPM1** — the cytoplasmic-mislocalized AML driver; its frameshift/insertion creates novel peptides presented on HLA, addressable by TCR/TCRm.
- **HPV E6/E7** — viral intracellular oncoproteins; a 2025 study built a TCRm **nanobody CAR-T** against HPV16 E6 on HLA-A\*02:01 with specific in vitro and in vivo killing. **Nanobodies' small size** lets them reach epitopes recessed in the MHC groove that conventional scFvs cannot — a genuine structural advantage for the cramped pMHC surface.

This is the bridge to the sibling report: TCR-mimics are the *display-dependent* answer to intracellular targets, and they fail exactly where display fails — which is why the [genotype-directed cytotoxicity](../opto-car/seed-report.md) program reads the **transcript** instead of the displayed peptide.

### HLA restriction: the fundamental ceiling

pMHC sensing inherits the single hardest limit in the field. Each TCRm or TCR product is locked to **one peptide on one HLA allele**. The dominant allele, HLA-A\*02:01, is present in only ~**27% of individuals of European descent at the rigorous allele-level estimate** (Tano-Menka et al., 2023, *JAMA Network Open*–reported eligibility analysis) — older haplotype-level figures of "~45–50% of Caucasians" reflect the broader HLA-A2 serotype, not the specific peptide-presenting allele — and substantially **lower in African (~12%) and many Asian populations**. Three structural consequences:

1. **Coverage inequity.** An A\*02:01-restricted product serves a minority of patients and, because allele frequencies vary by ancestry, *systematically* under-serves non-European populations — a documented driver of trial-eligibility disparity. Covering even most patients requires a *portfolio* of allele-matched products (a manufacturing and regulatory multiplier).
2. **Display dependence is a second gate.** Even in an HLA-matched patient, the peptide must be *generated and presented*: proteasomal cleavage preferences, TAP transport, peptide–MHC binding affinity, and protein abundance all gate whether the neoepitope ever reaches single-digit surface copies. **Loss-of-function and frameshift lesions are worst-case** — frameshifts make long novel reading frames (good neoantigen *potential*) but the transcripts are degraded by nonsense-mediated decay, throttling display.
3. **HLA loss is a built-in escape hatch.** Tumors routinely downregulate or delete MHC-I (β2-microglobulin mutation, HLA LOH) to evade T cells — the very mechanism that defeats checkpoint inhibitors also blinds *every* pMHC-directed therapy at once. (The mirror-image of this — exploiting HLA *loss* as a NOT-gate discriminator — is the A2 Bio Tmod logic, noted in the sibling report.)

> **HLA restriction is not an engineering bug to be optimized away; it is a thermodynamic and population-genetic ceiling on the entire surface-pMHC strategy. It is the single strongest argument for reading beneath the surface (Part III).**

### Programmable surface sensing: synNotch, SNIPR, MESA — the writable input layer

The most important conceptual advance in surface reading is the **decoupling of recognition from response**. Natural receptors hardwire input to a fixed output; synthetic receptors make the output a *free variable you transcribe*.

- **synNotch** (Morsut et al., 2016; Roybal et al., 2016, Lim lab) takes the Notch core — an extracellular ligand-binding domain (scFv/nanobody), the Notch regulatory/transmembrane region, and an intracellular **synthetic transcription factor** (e.g., Gal4-VP64 or tTA). Binding a **membrane-bound** antigen induces mechanically-gated proteolysis by ADAM and **γ-secretase**, releasing the TF, which drives *any* user-defined transgene. The output can be a CAR, a cytokine, a differentiation factor, a fluorophore — **arbitrary input → arbitrary output**, with no native signaling. This is the molecular substrate of cellular logic gates: synNotch(antigen A) → CAR(antigen B) implements **A-AND-B** combinatorial targeting, restoring the Signal-2-like contextual check the bare CAR deleted. It is the canonical answer to on-target/off-tumor toxicity.
- **SNIPR** (Synthetic Intramembrane Proteolysis Receptor; Zhu et al., 2022, *Cell*) is a re-engineered, humanized synNotch-class receptor with **markedly lower baseline leak and higher fold-induction**, and — critically — the ability to sense **soluble** ligands via a **pH-dependent endocytic cleavage** mechanism, broadening inputs beyond cell-contact-only.
- **MESA** (Modular Extracellular Sensor Architecture; Daringer et al., 2014; Schwarz et al., 2017, Leonard lab) uses a **ligand-induced dimerization** of two receptor chains that trans-cleave a tethered TF (or, in newer versions, reconstitute a split effector), and is geared toward **soluble** ligands — cytokines, growth factors, small molecules. A 2024 generation (Leonard lab, *Nature*) substantially improved soluble-ligand sensing for disease signals.

Mechanistically these split into two recognition physics: **synNotch/SNIPR require mechanical force** (a pulling event between bound ligand and receptor exposes the cleavage site — they are best at *membrane-bound, surface-displayed* ligands and thereby read *cell–cell context*), whereas **MESA reads soluble ligands** by dimerization (it reads the *microenvironment's diffusible state*). Together they give an engineered cell a programmable **input port** spanning surface and soluble cues — the literal "sense" layer of the treatise's sense→compute→act thesis, now writable.

The strategic frame: **a CAR is a fixed sensor-actuator; synNotch/SNIPR/MESA make sensing a programmable layer feeding a downstream circuit.** This is what lets the field move from single-antigen recognition to genuine multi-input logic, and it is the surface-domain precursor to the *intracellular* sensors (ADAR/Cas13) that Part III installs to read genotype rather than surface display.

## Counterintuitive & Groundbreaking Applications

- **Engineer affinity *down*, not up.** The commercial reflex — affinity-mature your binder — is, for cellular therapeutics, often value-destroying. Deliberately reduced-affinity CARs widen the tumor/normal density window (Liu et al., 2015) and are now a rational design axis, not a compromise. The clinical-development implication is a screen for the *kinetic sweet spot* (dwell-time-matched binders), not the tightest binder. A defensible IP and product wedge exists in **dwell-time-tuned binder libraries** characterized by k_off rather than K_D.
- **pMHC as a "publishing" channel for undruggable drivers.** KRAS G12D, mutant TP53, and NPM1 are the most-wanted oncology targets and are intracellular. The non-obvious move is that *the cell already publishes them to its surface as pMHC* — a TCRm/nanobody-CAR converts a private intracellular driver into a public, addressable surface antigen. The frontier is **public neoantigen × common-HLA** intersections (KRAS-G12D × HLA-A\*11:01 covers far more of East Asian populations than A\*02:01), turning HLA diversity from a liability into a portfolio map.
- **Density-thresholding as a programmable selectivity dial.** The Hernandez-Lopez two-receptor ultrasensitive circuit shows you can *design the sigmoid* — set the midpoint between normal-tissue and tumor antigen density. This makes previously-untouchable shared antigens (HER2, EGFR) addressable by *quantitative* rather than *qualitative* discrimination, opening solid tumors that lack a clean tumor-specific antigen.
- **synNotch as a context-restricted CAR-deployment system.** The boldest near-term clinical play is using synNotch(tissue/context antigen) → CAR(tumor antigen) to confine CAR expression *anatomically* — e.g., a brain-restricted antigen gating a CAR against a pan-tissue tumor antigen, so the CAR is only ever made in the CNS (Choe et al., 2021, *Sci Transl Med* — synNotch-gated glioblastoma targeting). This is genotype-blind but **context-aware** killing, and it is the surface-domain template for the *genotype-AND-context* logic that Part III aims at.

## Open Questions

- **Where is the true kinetic optimum, and is it patient-specific?** The proofreading sweet spot depends on TCR/CAR copy number, target density, and microenvironment. We lack a predictive map from binder k_off → in vivo selectivity, and whether the optimum shifts across patients/tumors is unknown.
- **Can pMHC display be made predictable enough to engineer against?** Proteasomal cleavage, TAP, and MHC-binding prediction are improving but still err; whether a given driver mutation yields a *reliably displayed* neoepitope at therapeutic copy number remains case-by-case. NMD's quantitative impact on frameshift-neoantigen display is poorly mapped.
- **Is HLA restriction soluble at all?** Pan-HLA or HLA-agnostic pMHC recognition is largely a fantasy today; multi-allele portfolios are the realistic path, but the manufacturing/regulatory cost of N parallel products is unaddressed.
- **How leaky is "leak-free"?** SNIPR and gen-2 synNotch report low baseline, but tonic signaling and baseline TF release set the floor on AND-gate fidelity. The quantitative leak budget tolerable for a *kill* output (vs a fluorescent reporter) is unestablished.
- **Does serial triggering survive in solid tumors?** The fast-k_off serial-triggering regime is characterized largely in vitro; whether it holds in the suppressive, low-perfusion solid-tumor synapse is open and bears directly on whether low-affinity CARs work where they are most needed.
