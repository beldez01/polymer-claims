# Part VIII — Delivery: Getting Programs Into the Right Cells

> **Filed under:** Programmable Living Medicines > Part VIII
> **Created:** 2026-06-09
> **Last updated:** 2026-06-09
> **Tags:** delivery, LNP, targeted-LNP, AAV, lentivirus, in-vivo-CAR, HSC-targeting, endosomal-escape, tropism

## Summary

If the program is the new unit of medicine, delivery is the syringe — and it is the part that does not yet work at scale. A therapeutic program (an mRNA-encoded CAR, a base editor, a sense-and-respond circuit) is, in isolation, almost free: synthesizing the nucleic acid is a commodity. What is scarce, and where the entire value of the field now concentrates, is the **address** — the molecular machinery that carries the program through blood, escapes the liver's gravitational pull, finds the one cell type in 10^13 that should receive it, gets inside, and releases the cargo into the cytosol before the endosome digests it. This chapter argues a single thesis from first principles: **the program is cheap, the address is the moat.** The evidence is commercial as much as scientific — AbbVie paid up to $2.1B for Capstan's targeted-LNP platform (AbbVie, 2025), Eli Lilly up to $7B for Kelonia's in-vivo CAR delivery (Lilly, 2026), Kite $350M for Interius's lentiviral in-vivo platform (Gilead, 2025) — and in every case the asset purchased was *delivery*, not payload. The brutal physics underneath: even the best lipid nanoparticles release only ~1–2% of their nucleic-acid cargo into the cytosol (Gilleron et al., 2013), the default biodistribution of an untargeted LNP is overwhelmingly to the liver, and the cells that matter most for durable cure — quiescent long-term hematopoietic stem cells — barely endocytose at all.

## Background / First Principles

Delivery is the product of four independent terms, and a failure in any one collapses the whole. Write the effective dose reaching the right intracellular compartment as:

**Effective program dose ≈ (targeting fidelity) × (payload protection) × (cellular uptake) × (endosomal escape) × (administered dose)**

Each is a separate engineering problem with its own ceiling.

**Targeting fidelity — the "address."** Left to itself, a systemically injected nanoparticle does not go where you want; it goes where the body's clearance machinery sends it. For lipid nanoparticles the default destination is the **liver**, because plasma apolipoprotein E (ApoE) adsorbs onto the particle surface and is recognized by the LDL receptor on hepatocytes — the same pathway that clears native lipoproteins (the mechanism Alnylam's patisiran/Onpattro exploits deliberately for hepatic siRNA delivery). For adeno-associated virus, destination is set by **capsid serotype tropism** (AAV8/AAV9 to liver/CNS/muscle, etc.). Reaching anything *extrahepatic* — a circulating T cell, a marrow stem cell, a tumor — means actively overriding this default. That override is the moat.

**Payload protection.** Naked mRNA has a serum half-life of minutes; naked DNA is chewed by nucleases and triggers innate sensors. The carrier must shield the cargo from degradation and from premature immune detection until it reaches the target. LNPs do this by encapsulation inside an ionizable-lipid core; viral vectors do it inside a protein capsid.

**Cellular uptake and endosomal escape — the two silent killers.** Even a correctly addressed, well-protected particle that binds the right cell must be *internalized* and then must get its cargo *out of the endosome* into the cytosol (for mRNA/RNP) or nucleus (for DNA/editing). Endocytosis delivers the particle into an acidifying vesicle that is on a conveyor belt to the lysosome. Escaping that vesicle is the rate-limiting biophysical step of the entire field — and it is staggeringly inefficient. Quantitative imaging of LNP-delivered siRNA found that **only ~1–2% of internalized siRNA escapes into the cytosol**; the other ~98% is recycled out or destroyed (Gilleron et al., 2013). Ionizable lipids are engineered specifically to attack this bottleneck: they are near-neutral at physiological pH (reducing toxicity and opsonization in circulation) but protonate in the acidifying endosome (~pH 6–6.5), where the resulting cationic charge disrupts the endosomal membrane and forces a fraction of cargo into the cytosol. The march from the first-generation ionizable lipid **DLin-MC3-DMA** (the lipid in patisiran) to the mRNA-vaccine lipids **SM-102** (Moderna/Spikevax) and **ALC-0315** (Pfizer–BioNTech/Comirnaty) was largely a campaign to raise this escape fraction and tune biodegradability — yet even the best clinical lipids remain in the low-single-digit-percent escape regime.

**Dose and its ceiling.** You can brute-force low efficiency with more particles, but every modality has a toxicity ceiling. For AAV, systemic doses in the range of ~10^13–10^14 vector genomes/kg used for muscle and CNS indications have produced serious and occasionally fatal complement activation, hepatotoxicity, and thrombotic microangiopathy — capsid dose *is* the toxicity. Pre-existing neutralizing antibodies to common AAV serotypes (present in a large fraction of the adult population) further cap the deliverable dose and frequently exclude patients outright. So "just give more" is not a general escape hatch.

The four-component LNP formulation that recurs across the field is worth stating explicitly, because the ratios are load-bearing: an **ionizable lipid** (the escape engine), a **helper phospholipid** (e.g., DSPC, structural), **cholesterol** (membrane stability/fusion), and a **PEG-lipid** (controls particle size and circulation time; typically only ~1.5 mol%, because too much PEG blocks uptake). A representative mRNA-LNP molar ratio is on the order of **ionizable:DSPC:cholesterol:PEG ≈ 50:10:38.5:1.5**, with an ionizable-lipid-to-mRNA mass (N/P) ratio in the single digits. These four numbers, more than the payload sequence, determine where the particle goes and whether anything gets out of the endosome.

## Key Findings

### Targeted LNPs (tLNPs): buying an extrahepatic address

The defining move of the last five years is decorating the LNP surface with a **targeting ligand** — an antibody, antibody fragment, or nanobody — to redirect it away from the hepatic default toward a chosen cell surface marker. The proof-of-concept that lit the field came from the Epstein lab: **anti-CD5-targeted LNPs carrying CAR-encoding mRNA, injected into mice with cardiac fibrosis, generated functional, *transient* anti-fibrotic CAR-T cells in vivo**, reduced fibrosis, and restored cardiac function — without ever removing a cell from the body (Rurik et al., Science 2022). Two features make this a template for programmable living medicine: the CAR was **transient** (mRNA is non-integrating and decays over days, an intrinsic off-switch and safety feature), and the manufacturing happened *inside the patient*, collapsing the ex-vivo cell-engineering supply chain entirely.

This template is now the commercial frontier. **Capstan Therapeutics**, founded on University of Pennsylvania tLNP IP, advanced **CPTX2309**, an in-vivo anti-CD19 CAR-T candidate generated by tLNP-delivered mRNA, into Phase 1 for B-cell-mediated autoimmune disease — and AbbVie acquired the company for **up to $2.1B** in June 2025 (AbbVie press release, 2025), explicitly to obtain the "CellSeeker" tLNP platform. The payload (an anti-CD19 CAR) is textbook; the value was the address.

A complementary, ligand-free route to extrahepatic targeting is **Selective Organ Targeting (SORT)**: adding a fifth, charged "SORT" lipid to the four-component formulation systematically shifts biodistribution. Cheng et al. (Nat Nanotechnol 2020) showed that adding a permanently cationic lipid (e.g., DOTAP) redirects delivery to **lung**, an anionic lipid (e.g., 18PA) to **spleen**, and an ionizable-only formulation stays in **liver** — and that this works for mRNA, Cas9 mRNA/sgRNA, and even Cas9 ribonucleoprotein, enabling organ-specific CRISPR editing. SORT demonstrated that biodistribution is a *tunable* property of lipid composition, not a fixed fate.

### AAV, lentivirus, and engineered VLPs: the viral toolbox and its limits

**AAV** remains the workhorse for *in vivo* gene addition where the cargo is small and the target tissue is AAV-tropic. Its virtues: it is largely **episomal/integration-free** (low insertional-mutagenesis risk) and durable in non-dividing tissue. Its hard ceilings: a packaging limit of **~4.7 kb** (which cannot hold a full-length Cas9 plus guide and regulatory elements — forcing split-intein or smaller-nuclease workarounds), widespread **pre-existing immunity** that excludes seropositive patients, and **dose-dependent toxicity** at the high systemic doses some indications demand.

**Lentivirus** integrates and is the clinical-grade backbone for *ex vivo* engineering of T cells and CD34+ HSCs. The field's most striking recent move is taking lentivirus *in vivo*: **Umoja Biopharma's VivoVec** pairs a third-generation lentiviral vector with a surface T-cell-targeting-and-activation complex so that T cells manufacture their own CAR in situ; **UB-VV111** (in-situ anti-CD19 CAR-T) cleared its IND in July 2024 and entered the Phase 1 INVICTA-1 trial (NCT06528301) (Umoja, 2024). **Interius BioTherapeutics** built an analogous lentiviral in-vivo CAR platform (lead **INT2104**) and was acquired by Gilead's **Kite for $350M** in August 2025 (Gilead, 2025) — again, a delivery acquisition.

**Engineered virus-like particles (eVLPs)** occupy a clever middle ground: they borrow viral assembly and entry machinery but carry **protein, not nucleic acid**, delivering a base editor or Cas9 as a transient ribonucleoprotein that leaves no encoding DNA behind. Banskota et al. (Cell 2022) engineered fourth-generation eVLPs that achieved **up to ~95% base editing in cultured cells** and, from a single injection in mice, **~63% liver editing and a 78% reduction in serum PCSK9**, plus partial rescue in a model of inherited blindness. eVLPs marry the transience of mRNA-LNP with the cargo-protection of a capsid — a delivery primitive purpose-built for editors.

### Ex vivo vs in vivo: the trade space that decides COGS

The cleanest way to see why delivery is the rate-limiter is to compare the two manufacturing topologies.

**Ex vivo** delivery — apheresing a patient's cells, transducing them in a clean room, expanding, and reinfusing — is *clinically solved*. Lentiviral transduction of CD34+ HSCs and T cells is routine; the FDA-approved CRISPR HSC therapy **Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel)** for sickle-cell disease and β-thalassemia is the existence proof that you can edit a human's stem cells outside the body and put them back curatively. But ex vivo is a *bespoke per-patient factory*: weeks of vein-to-vein time, lymphodepleting conditioning, six-to-seven-figure cost of goods, and a supply chain that does not scale to common disease.

**In vivo** delivery is the frontier precisely because it **collapses COGS**. If a single IV infusion of a targeted particle can engineer the cell in situ, the bespoke factory disappears, conditioning may be avoidable, and the therapy becomes an off-the-shelf biologic. Every major deal cited above is a bet that the field's value migrates from ex-vivo manufacturing to in-vivo delivery. The entire economic argument of programmable living medicine reduces to: *can you address the cell in vivo well enough to retire the clean room?*

### The marrow problem: reaching the quiescent long-term HSC

The hardest cell in the body to deliver to is also one of the most therapeutically valuable: the **long-term hematopoietic stem cell (LT-HSC)**. LT-HSCs are rare, deeply quiescent, transcriptionally quiet, and — fatally for nanoparticle delivery — they barely endocytose. A dormant stem cell does not pull particles in at the rate a cycling lymphoma cell does, so the uptake term in the delivery equation craters exactly where durable cure requires it (a single surviving mutant LT-HSC can reseed a clonal disease).

The most important recent result against this wall is **anti-CD117 (c-Kit) antibody-targeted LNP**. Breda, Shi, and colleagues showed that a single IV dose of c-Kit-targeted LNP delivered mRNA to HSPCs in vivo, and at higher dose (~20 µg mRNA) transfected **~90% of HSPCs and LT-HSCs**; Cre mRNA delivery edited ~90% of HSPCs while preserving stemness, and the platform enabled near-complete correction in a sickle-cell model plus *non-genotoxic conditioning* via pro-apoptotic PUMA mRNA (Breda et al., Science 2023; reporter/delivery work in Nano Lett 2023). Two cautions the treatise must keep honest: that ~90% figure is **reporter/cargo delivery at high dose, not durable editing efficiency**, and CD117 doubles as an HSC-depletion address — a feature (built-in conditioning) and a hazard (on-target stem toxicity). Parallel work from the Weissman group established T-cell-homing tLNPs — e.g., engineered **CD4-targeted** mRNA-LNPs that drove efficient recombination in CD4+ T cells in vivo (Tombácz et al., Mol Ther 2021) — extending the ligand-targeting logic across the hematopoietic compartment toward CD45/CD8 addresses now in development.

A biological trick complements the molecular one: **wake the stem cell before you knock**. Interferon-α drives dormant LT-HSCs into cycle (Essers et al., Nature 2009); cycling cells transcribe and endocytose more, raising both the target-transcript signal and the LNP-uptake term simultaneously. In the JAK2-V617F MPN setting this is doubly favorable because IFN-α *preferentially* activates and depletes the mutant clone (Mullally et al., Blood 2013). The combined protocol — IFN-α priming pulse → CD117/CD45-targeted LNP delivery into the now-cycling (preferentially mutant) LT-HSC → genotype-gated effector — is developed at length in the sibling report on genotype-directed cytotoxicity (see [Connections](#connections)). For delivery specifically, the lesson is that **priming converts the marrow's worst delivery substrate into a tractable one**, but only transiently and never to 100% of the deepest-dormant pool.

### First-principles: what fraction of cells must you actually transfect?

The required delivery efficiency is set by the *indication*, and the numbers differ by orders of magnitude — a fact that reorders which programs are buildable today.

- **Liver/secreted-protein indications** (e.g., editing hepatocytes to knock down PCSK9 or TTR): you need only a *fraction* of hepatocytes edited, because the therapeutic output is a secreted or systemically-sensed protein and the liver is the default LNP destination anyway. This is why hepatic editing (Intellia's NTLA-2001 for ATTR, with ~50%+ serum TTR knockdown from one infusion) is the most mature in-vivo editing application — the address is *free*.
- **Clonal hematopoiesis / stem-cell cure**: you need to reach a very high fraction of the **self-renewing LT-HSC** pool, because any unedited mutant stem cell can repopulate the clone. This is the harshest requirement in the field — high efficiency in the lowest-uptake cell — and is why marrow delivery is the rate-limiter for the whole hematologic ambition.
- **Solid tumor**: an intermediate, heterogeneous requirement gated less by per-cell efficiency than by *penetration* through stroma and interstitial pressure; many tumor cells must be reached, but a fully transfected core is not required if the effector recruits bystander immunity.

The unifying point: **the same payload can be trivially deliverable for one indication and effectively impossible for another, purely because of the address.** Delivery, not biology of the program, picks the winners.

## Counterintuitive & Groundbreaking Applications

**1. The program is cheap; the address is the moat — and the M&A market has already priced it.** The single most counterintuitive fact for a molecule-trained intuition is that in three landmark 2025–2026 acquisitions (Capstan/AbbVie ~$2.1B, Kelonia/Lilly ~$7B, Interius/Kite $350M) the asset purchased was the **delivery vehicle**, while the payloads (anti-CD19, anti-BCMA CARs) were near-commodity. Value in programmable living medicine concentrates not at the instruction set but at the *targeting and escape* machinery. A founder's corollary: own a defensible address (a tropism, a ligand, an escape chemistry) and you can swap payloads at will; own only a payload and you are a tenant on someone else's delivery platform.

**2. Transience as a feature, not a bug.** mRNA-LNP CAR-T is *worse* than integrated CAR-T at persistence — and that is exactly why it may be safer and more drug-like. A transient in-vivo CAR (Rurik et al., 2022) self-limits: the CAR decays in days, giving a built-in off-switch for autoimmune and fibrotic indications where you want to *reset* a tissue, not permanently re-arm the immune system. The "weakness" of mRNA becomes the safety differentiator that makes in-vivo CAR-T deployable in non-malignant disease — the multi-billion-dollar autoimmune market Capstan and Kelonia are aimed at.

**3. Lipid composition as a programmable ZIP code.** SORT (Cheng et al., 2020) reframes biodistribution from destiny to *parameter*: a single added charged lipid moves the particle between lung, spleen, and liver. The frontier implication is a **combinatorial delivery design space** where the address is encoded in the *formulation*, not (or in addition to) a protein ligand — vastly cheaper to manufacture and iterate than antibody conjugation. The counterintuitive corollary: you may not need a targeting antibody at all if the lipid chemistry alone biases the particle to the right organ.

**4. Built-in conditioning collapses the transplant.** The c-Kit-LNP result that the *same* address (CD117) can both deliver a corrective program *and*, with a PUMA payload, provide non-genotoxic conditioning (Breda et al., 2023) hints at a future where HSC gene therapy needs neither busulfan nor irradiation — the delivery vehicle does the conditioning. That single change would move stem-cell cure from a hospital-bound, toxic procedure to an infusion, and is arguably the highest-leverage unsolved problem in the field.

**5. Wake-then-deliver: pharmacology timing as a delivery primitive.** Using IFN-α to push quiescent (preferentially mutant) LT-HSCs into cycle *before* dosing the LNP (Essers 2009; Mullally 2013) treats the cell's *cell-cycle state* as a tunable delivery variable. This is delivery engineering by pharmacology rather than chemistry — a conceptually distinct lever that turns the marrow's worst delivery substrate into a temporarily addressable one, and sets a master clock the rest of the regimen times to.

## Open Questions

- **Can endosomal escape be pushed past ~1–2%?** This is the single biophysical ceiling that, if raised an order of magnitude, would lower every dose and widen every therapeutic window in the field. It remains stubbornly low despite a decade of lipid engineering.
- **How high can in-vivo LT-HSC *editing* (not reporter delivery) actually go, durably?** The ~90% c-Kit-LNP figure is cargo delivery at high dose; the editing-and-engraftment number that matters for cure is unproven.
- **Does the AAV dose-toxicity ceiling have a clean engineering fix** (de-immunized capsids, evolved tropism, pre-dosing tolerance), or is it a hard wall for systemic indications?
- **Will ligand-targeted tLNPs hold their address in humans,** where the protein corona, ApoE competition, and Fc-receptor uptake by liver and spleen macrophages are far more aggressive than in mice? Most striking tropism results are murine.
- **Re-dosing and anti-PEG / anti-vector immunity:** transient modalities may require repeat dosing, but anti-PEG antibodies and anti-capsid immunity accumulate. How many cycles before the address stops working?
