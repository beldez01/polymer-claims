# Genome Editing and Targeted Genome Engineering: A Technique Compendium

> **Filed under:** Synthetic Biology > Genome Editing
> **Created:** 2026-07-04
> **Last updated:** 2026-07-04
> **Tags:** genome-editing, CRISPR, base-editing, prime-editing, recombineering, integrases, off-target-detection, synthetic-biology

## Summary
Targeted genome engineering has progressed from protein-guided nucleases (meganucleases, ZFNs, TALENs) to RNA-guided systems (CRISPR-Cas9, Cas12a, Cas13) and, most recently, to precision editors that write defined changes without relying on double-strand breaks (base editors, prime editors) or that install large payloads via recombinases and CRISPR-associated transposases. This report catalogs the major technique families by mechanism, use case, and trade-offs, alongside the delivery modalities and off-target detection assays that determine whether a given editor is usable in cells, animals, or the clinic. Every citation below has been verified against the primary literature with DOIs.

## Background
The unifying principle of targeted genome engineering is the creation of a programmable sequence-recognition event at a chosen genomic locus. Early tools achieved recognition through protein-DNA interfaces that had to be re-engineered for each target; the field's inflection point was the discovery that a nucleic acid guide could replace protein recognition, making retargeting a matter of synthesizing a new oligonucleotide. A second inflection came when researchers decoupled targeting from double-strand-break (DSB) generation: because DSBs are repaired stochastically (yielding indels via non-homologous end joining) and can cause translocations, large deletions, and p53 activation, the newest editors deploy nicking or nick-free chemistry to install precise edits. In parallel, bacterial recombination machinery (lambda Red, retrons, site-specific recombinases, Tn7 transposons) provides an orthogonal route to insert, delete, or replace sequence, especially for multiplexed and large-cargo applications.

## Key Findings

### Protein-guided nucleases: meganucleases, ZFNs, TALENs
**Meganucleases** (homing endonucleases such as I-SceI) recognize long (14-40 bp) asymmetric sites and cut with high specificity, but their intertwined DNA-recognition and catalytic functions make retargeting difficult; engineering campaigns must redesign the enzyme scaffold itself [1]. **Zinc finger nucleases (ZFNs)** fuse arrays of Cys2-His2 zinc fingers (each reading ~3 bp) to the FokI cleavage domain, which must dimerize to cut, giving paired left/right half-sites. Urnov and colleagues demonstrated highly efficient correction of an endogenous human gene (IL2RG) by ZFN-stimulated homology-directed repair, establishing designer nucleases as a practical editing platform [2]. **TALENs** exploit the modular code of Xanthomonas transcription activator-like effectors, where two hypervariable residues (the repeat-variable diresidue) in each 34-aa repeat specify a single base pair [3]. Fusing TALE arrays to FokI produced easily retargetable nucleases [4], and optimized architectures with defined N/C-terminal truncations and spacer lengths made TALENs robust and accessible [5]. Strengths: excellent specificity and, for TALENs, near-arbitrary targeting; limits: laborious protein engineering and larger cargo size than CRISPR.

### CRISPR-Cas9: the RNA-guided revolution
Jinek et al. showed that Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 is a dual-RNA-guided (crRNA:tracrRNA) DNA endonuclease that can be reprogrammed with a single fused guide RNA (sgRNA), and that its RuvC and HNH nuclease domains cut the two DNA strands to make a blunt DSB adjacent to a PAM [6]. Gasiunas et al. independently established the Cas9-crRNA ribonucleoprotein cleavage mechanism the same year [7]. Within months, Cong et al. and Mali et al. demonstrated multiplexable Cas9 editing in mammalian cells, launching CRISPR as a general genome-engineering tool [8,9]. Strengths: trivial retargeting, multiplexing via multiple sgRNAs, low cost; limits: NGG PAM requirement, DSB-associated repair outcomes, and off-target cleavage.

### Cas variants: fidelity, PAM range, and orthogonal effectors
Structure- and rational-engineering efforts reduced off-target activity by weakening nonspecific Cas9-DNA contacts: **eSpCas9** (Slaymaker et al.) neutralizes positively charged residues in the nontarget-strand groove [10], and **SpCas9-HF1** (Kleinstiver et al.) removes DNA-backbone hydrogen bonds, achieving no detectable genome-wide off-targets for many guides [11]. **SpCas9-NG** (Nishimasu et al.) relaxes the PAM to NG, roughly doubling the targetable space while retaining wild-type-like specificity [12]. **Cas-CLOVER** (Madison et al.) is a dimerization-dependent high-fidelity nuclease pairing catalytically dead Cas9 with the Clo051 nuclease domain, so cleavage requires two adjacent guide-defined sites—useful for clean multiplexed editing of primary T cells [13].

### Cas12a/Cpf1 and Cas13 (RNA targeting)
**Cas12a (Cpf1)** is a single-RNA-guided class 2 effector that recognizes a T-rich PAM, processes its own crRNA array (simplifying multiplexing), and generates staggered cuts with 5' overhangs [14]. **Cas13** effectors are RNA-guided RNA endonucleases: Abudayyeh et al. adapted LwaCas13a for programmable transcript knockdown in mammalian and plant cells [15], and Cox et al. built the catalytically dead dCas13-based **REPAIR** system, fusing an ADAR deaminase to perform programmable A-to-I (read as G) RNA editing without touching the genome [16]. RNA targeting offers reversible, DNA-scar-free perturbation, at the cost of transient effect and collateral cleavage considerations.

### Base editing: point mutations without DSBs
Base editors fuse a Cas9 nickase to a single-stranded-DNA deaminase, converting one base pair to another within a small activity window. **Cytosine base editors (CBEs)** (Komor et al.) use a cytidine deaminase plus uracil glycosylase inhibitor to install C•G→T•A edits without a DSB [17]. **Adenine base editors (ABEs)** (Gaudelli et al.) required directed evolution of a DNA-active adenine deaminase (from a tRNA enzyme) to achieve A•T→G•C conversions [18]. Together CBEs and ABEs address the majority of pathogenic point mutations; limits include bystander edits within the window, sequence-context constraints, and deaminase-driven off-target DNA/RNA editing.

### Prime editing and large-insertion methods
Prime editing (Anzalone et al.) fuses a Cas9 nickase to an engineered reverse transcriptase and uses a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) whose 3' extension both primes synthesis and templates the desired edit, enabling all 12 point substitutions plus small insertions/deletions without DSBs or donor DNA [19]. **Twin prime editing (twinPE)** uses two pegRNAs to rewrite larger sequences and, combined with a serine recombinase, to integrate, invert, or replace kilobase-scale segments [20]. **PASTE** (Yarnall et al.) marries prime editing to a serine integrase: the prime editor writes an attachment site into the genome, and the co-delivered integrase then inserts payloads up to ~36 kb, achieving programmable large insertions in dividing and non-dividing human cells [21]. These methods trade higher molecular complexity for markedly reduced reliance on error-prone DSB repair.

### CRISPRi and CRISPRa: transcriptional control with dCas9
Catalytically dead Cas9 (dCas9) becomes a programmable DNA-binding scaffold. **CRISPRi** (Qi et al.) uses dCas9, optionally fused to a KRAB repressor, to block transcription initiation or elongation, giving reversible, specific, multiplexable knockdown [22]. Genome-scale libraries pairing dCas9-repressor and dCas9-activator (**CRISPRa**, e.g., VP64/SAM systems) enabled systematic loss- and gain-of-function screens across the genome [23]. Strengths: reversibility, tunability, no permanent genomic change; limits: incomplete knockdown/activation and promoter-proximal targeting rules.

### Recombineering, MAGE, and multiplexed genome engineering
Bacterial recombineering uses phage recombination proteins—classically the lambda Red (Beta/Exo/Gam) system—to integrate PCR products or oligonucleotides via short homology arms; Datsenko and Wanner's one-step gene inactivation method became a workhorse for E. coli engineering [24]. **MAGE** (Wang et al.) scales single-stranded-oligonucleotide recombineering into automated cycles that simultaneously modify many loci across a population, accelerating combinatorial genome optimization (demonstrated by tuning a lycopene pathway) [25]. These approaches excel at genome-wide, population-level diversification in microbes rather than single-allele precision in mammalian cells.

### Site-specific recombinases, integrases, transposons, and retrons
**Large serine integrases** such as phiC31 and Bxb1 catalyze unidirectional recombination between orthogonal attB/attP sites, permitting stable, irreversible insertion; Groth et al. showed phiC31 integrase functions efficiently in human cells, seeding its use as a genomic "landing pad" tool [26]—Bxb1 is now the integrase of choice in twinPE and PASTE workflows [20,21]. **CRISPR-associated transposases (CAST)** co-opt Tn7-like transposons for RNA-guided insertion: Strecker et al. characterized the type V-K ShCAST (Cas12k-directed) system [27] and Klompe et al. described a type I-F Vibrio cholerae system that directs RNA-guided DNA integration at a fixed distance from the target [28]. These enable programmable, DSB-independent insertion in bacteria, with active engineering toward eukaryotic use. **Retron-based editing** (retron library recombineering) uses a retron's reverse transcriptase to produce editing ssDNA in vivo, coupling recombineering to high-throughput, trackable variant screens beyond the scale of CRISPR selection [29].

### Delivery considerations
The editor's molecular size and the target tissue dictate delivery. **AAV** offers efficient in vivo transduction but its ~4.7 kb packaging limit forces split-intein or dual-vector strategies for large editors (SpCas9, base/prime editors). **Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)** deliver transient Cas9 mRNA plus sgRNA, minimizing genomic-integration risk and enabling redosing; the NTLA-2001 trial (Gillmore et al.) achieved durable in vivo TTR knockdown in patients via LNP-delivered CRISPR, the first systemic in vivo CRISPR therapeutic [30]. **Electroporation** of ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complexes gives fast, DNA-free, low-persistence editing ideal for ex vivo cell therapies (e.g., CAR-T, HSC editing). The general principle: transient RNP/mRNA delivery lowers off-target and immunogenicity risk relative to constitutive DNA/viral expression.

### Off-target detection methods
Empirical, genome-wide nomination of off-target sites is essential for both research and clinical translation. **GUIDE-seq** (Tsai et al.) tags nuclease-induced DSBs in living cells by capturing an integrated double-stranded oligo, then sequencing the tagged loci [31]. **CIRCLE-seq** (Tsai et al.) is a highly sensitive, cell-free assay that circularizes genomic DNA and detects in vitro Cas9 cleavage, requiring no reference genome and surfacing sites that in-cell assays may miss [32]. **DISCOVER-seq** (Wienert et al.) exploits the endogenous repair factor MRE11 by ChIP-sequencing its recruitment to cut sites, enabling unbiased off-target discovery in vivo and in patient-derived contexts [33]. In practice these assays are complementary—in vitro methods (CIRCLE-seq) maximize sensitivity, while cell/in vivo methods (GUIDE-seq, DISCOVER-seq) report physiologically relevant events.

## Open Questions
- Can prime editing, PASTE, and CAST reach therapeutically sufficient efficiency for large, precise insertions in non-dividing primary human cells and in vivo?
- How can eukaryotic CRISPR-associated transposition be made efficient and specific enough to rival integrases for programmable insertion?
- What is the true clinical significance of deaminase-driven genome-wide and transcriptome-wide off-target editing from base editors, and how completely do engineered deaminases eliminate it?
- Which delivery modality best balances transient exposure, tissue tropism, redosing, and immunogenicity for editors too large for single AAV?
- Can any single off-target assay serve as a regulatory-grade standard, or will orthogonal assay panels remain necessary?
- How do chromatin state and cell-cycle phase govern editing outcomes, and can they be controlled to bias repair toward precise products?
